TheMongol Empireis areformablecountryinAsia. It can only be formed by theGolden Horde, theIlkhanate, or theYuanwhich are also formable nations and have to be formed first and the Mongol Empire itself can also only be formed if the forming country has Tatar or Altaic primary culture. Reforming the Empire will also grant the forming country a unique government type, which is an upgrade from theSteppe nomadgovernment type.

The Mongol Empire has no unique national idea set and will simply inherit the national ideas from the nation that forms it. The Mongol Empire also gains access to a decision to release theIlkhanateas a march, even if it was the original founding nation.

One benefit from forming the Mongol Empire is that one gains specialisedbannerstroops in all provinces with your primary culture. This can create an unstoppable army; powerful, yet not a drain on your manpower or your economy. Banners have+5%discipline, cost only25%manpower to reinforce and their maintenance cost is-50%the maintenance of their equivalent non-banner regiments.

The Lions of the North DLCintroduced an alternative way to form theMongol Empire. It can be formed by any country with theHoly Horde government reform (acquired through theTeutonic Ordercrusader mission tree). You don't need to culture convert for this. Doing it this way will not change the government toGreat Mongol Stateand also not enablebanners.

### Strategy[edit|edit source]

AsGreat Horde, start by allyingUzbek, recruiting a mercenary company (this gets you over the force limit), and annexingKazan,Crimea,Ryazan,Nogai, andGazikumukh. (Much of the time, Kazan and Crimea start allied to each other, and, with careful maneuvering, it's possible to declare on both in December 1444 and to defeat both, annexing Crimea and at least a third of Kazan.)

Take advantage of your strengths (hard-hitting army, high cavalry ratio, and the ability to declare war on neighbors at will without the need to bother with spy operations) to aggressively expand, while keeping an eye on your finances (your income is one of your major weaknesses, and, since you don't have a Merchant Guilds estate, you can't even get cheap loans.)

If possible, allyGenoaor at least establish them as tributary, since that is the easiest way to get Renaissance (with luck, you can embrace Renaissance by 1465 without even getting into debt.) If you're successful, don't form any states (keep newly conquered provinces as territories) until you've embraced Renaissance.

Once a year, you'll get an event informing you that Muscovy refuses to pay tribute and asking you to raid them. Check their provinces for loot before deciding. It's most practical to raid them once every 3 years or so, since it takes 2 years after each raid for the province to start accumulating fresh loot.

Continue by pushing south across the Caucasus, with occasional pushes in other directions. Your primary focus should be on seizing the eastern half ofQara Qoyunlu,Ajam, andTimurids, and keeping these provinces out of Ottomans' grabby hands, while at the same time keeping youraggressive expansionin check and not aggravating your neighbors too severely. Try to restore the Golden Horde by 1500 or so. Once Uzbek and Muscovy are gone, start pushing east.Mingwill look formidable at first, but, once you destroyOirat, Ming will have the "unguarded nomadic frontier" disaster that leads to its disintegration, and, with a few military campaigns, you'll be able to seize Mongolia and the three Chinese provinces (Beijing, Nanjing, and Canton) required to form the Mongol Empire. (WARNING: the disaster requires a direct border with you; a border with your subject, such as Yuan, does not count. So, don't release Yuan too early.)

Don't neglectTransoxiana: although it might look small, it possesses a number of high-development provinces and you'll probably need to go to war with them several times before you can annex all the provinces you need. Keep an eye on the province of Hormuz, since it is also needed and it'll be hard to conquer without having a navy in the Persian Gulf.

One useful technique you can employ is repeated use of "return core province". This involves vassalizing a country with lots of cores in its neighbors, allying said neighbors, and using favors to get these cores back. You can do this first withKazakh: you'll be able to release Kazakh as a vassal when you conquer Nogai and and complete the "People of Kazakh" mission, and Kazakh has cores in nearly a half of Uzbek's starting territory. Later on, it'll prove very useful if you can manage to vassalizeKhorasan, possibly by getting someone to release it in a peace deal, it likewise has lots of cores throughout the region.

Finally, keep an eye on your two big neighbors, Lithuania and Ottomans. You'll need to go to war with Lithuania at some point, because forming the Mongol Empire requires four provinces that start in Lithuanian hands. This is best done before they form the Commonwealth and before there is a Defender of Catholic Faith (ideally, you'll want to ally Poland early on, so you can fight Lithuania without also fighting Poland.)

Ottomans will hate you from the moment you annex Crimea, and will remain a persistent threat. Maintain some strong allies to guard against them. Fortunately, if you play your cards right, you'll be able to match them militarily and even outrank them as a Great Power. Strike if you see an opportunity. You don't need any of their land (unless they manage to grab Theodoro or Genoese provinces on the Azov Sea early on), but you want to keep them back, and you can shake them down for a lot of gold in the peace deal. Take advantage of their major weakness: while they are busy besieging your forts in the Caucasus and in Persia, a lightning raid through Moldavia on Kostantiniyye by an army with a high Siege leader (and, if you're up to 7 in Military Tech, with a bunch of artillery) will often capture their capital before they can scramble their forces back.
