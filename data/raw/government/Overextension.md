### Source[edit|edit source]

Each non-coreprovince causes overextension equal to80%of its development[1]. For example, a province with a development of 10 will add 8% overextension regardless of how big the country is. Overextension has no cap; it can potentially go far beyond 100% and its penalties will continue to increase. #### Overextension impact[edit|edit source]

The impact of overextension on your nation is controlled by theoverextension impact modifier.

Certain nations have ideas to reduce this impact:

- Great Moravian idea 1: Reforge Moravia's Hegemony
- Eranian ambition
- Nagpuri ambition
#### Overextension reduction[edit|edit source]

Overextension can be reduced by:

- Coringprovinces, which takes time andadministrative power
- Selling or granting non-cored provinces to subjects to let the subjects core them (you may be able to annex them later)
- Selling provinces to other nations
- Releasing a vassal that has a core on it
- Making a newclient statefrom the non-cored provinces
Note that a country will only accept buying a province if they have a core on the province, or it has the same culture group and religion. They also should not be overextended themselves.

### Administrative efficiency[edit|edit source]

Each1%administrative efficiency has the following effects:

- −1%Cost of creating cores
- −1%Annexation cost
- −1%Warscore cost of provinces
- −1%Base aggressive expansion
- −1%Overextension
- +0.2%Liberty desirefor colonies, of the colonial nation's administrative efficiency.
All effects of administrative efficiency are capped at90%. Except for liberty desire, these are not additive modifiers, e.g. the effect on province warscore cost is not an addition to the modifierprovince warscore cost. Rather, they are appliedmultiplicatively, after modifiers doing the same thing.
